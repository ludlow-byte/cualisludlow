Evaluation of the National Park Service Website for Gettysburg National Military Park

by Andrew Shaw and Erin Ludlow, May 5, 2026

Part 1: Description of the Organization and Information System

What is the Gettysburg National Military Park

The Gettysburg Military Park is the preserved battlefield of the Battle of Gettysburg on July 1-3, 1863. The Battle of Gettysburg is a three-day battle that took place during the American Civil War (1861-1865), where the Union forces and the Confederacy fought, and the Union won. Gettysburg is considered the “high water mark of the Confederacy” because it’s the farthest north the Confederacy got.

Information System Content

The primary goal of this website is to provide basic information to potential tourists and professional and amateur historians. The website is well organized for this purpose. It has three sections in the center-left of most screens: Plan your visit, Learn more about the Park, and Get involved. On the right side, there are sections about fees, maps, and basic information about the park. On the main page, there are links to the visitor center, guided tours, stories of people who lived through the battle, places that make up the battlefield, and 3D tours. The links are updated regularly to show seasonal images and, in the case of calendar events, to move the list of events forward with the year. These links attract attention using both images and text. The main page also provides two opportunities to download the NPS app. The Visitor Center page is helpful, as it provides policies ranging from pets to firearms and includes sections on amenities. You also have the National Park's contact information. The website is accessible to all users, with no paywall, and visitors can take a tour of the battlefield without leaving the house. Users can also give feedback about the website via a form that asks whether it is helpful. At the top right, there is a menu link to the national National Park Service website.

Part 2: Evaluation of Existing Information System Using Nielsen’s Heuristics

Pros

Match Between the System and the Real World - The menu labels use simple language or familiar icons with text. Alerts for attraction closures and events are prominently displayed. Showcasing an image of the McPherson barn from roughly the time of the battle on the home page helps the website’s visitors identify with the physical park (the barn is a well-known landmark) and with the historical context of the website.

User Control and Freedom - The menu labels and alerts are available on every page, making navigation easy across the site. There are two links for home based on whether the user wants to go to the Gettysburg home page or the National Park Service home page, making exploration of the whole NPS system easy. The user does not have to create an account to access the website

Consistency and Standards - Menus, icons and links all use words, phrases or images that are relatively standard for tourism destinations, e.g. “Plan your visit”, “Visitors Center”, or “Where to Stay”

Flexibility and Efficiency of Use - Most child pages in the hierarchy can be reached through multiple paths. The menu labels, available on every page, use simple language or familiar icons with text. Alerts for attraction closures and events are prominently displayed.

Aesthetic and Minimalist Design - The Gettysburg website uses the same color scheme and format as the national website for the NPS and the other park sites.

Cons

Match Between the System and the Real World - The page for park statistics provides links to other websites instead of presenting them on Gettysburg’s website. It is a con because it lacks transparency. Another con in this category is the language used in the Superintendent Compendium page, which is extremely technical, and the section is extremely long.

Recognition Rather than Recall - Information about accessibility is spread across a couple of pages. Majority of the accessibility information for the park is available under the “plan your visit” drop-down menu, except for the Visitors Center. A Visitors Center link takes users to a different website for the Gettysburg Foundation, which runs the Visitors Center (not the NPS). If users navigate to the NPS version of the Visitors Center page, the “Accessibility and Safety” section does not include any accessibility information. And there’s no link on the NPS Version of the Visitors Center page to the Gettysburg Foundation Visitors Center page. Easy, one-stop-access to accessibility information is important because of the number visitors per year and because of the prevalence of senior citizen visitors who may need additional assistance navigating the Park’s facilities.

Help and Documentation - Using the Search field to search for “FAQ” yields no results. The user must type “Frequently Asked Questions” in the Search field to find what they’re looking for.

Part 3: Critical Analysis and Recommendations

Suggestions to Improve the “Cons”

Match Between the System and the Real World - The language in some sections of the website is too technical and needs simplifications. Sections like the Compendium need to be short and concise, allowing users to understand the key points. Another section that needs improvement is the statistics section. The NPS should include statistics numbers instead of links to show transparency to users.

Recognition Rather than Recall - The NPS Visitor Center page should include a link to the Gettysburg Foundation Visitor Center website, and vice versa. Both pages should have the same set of “Accessibility & Safety” information: Accessibility, Tick Safety, Pets, Firearms, Bicycles, and Bags. A link should be prominently placed for a map of the park with all of the restrooms marked.”

Benefits to the Gettysburg Park From These Changes

During our perusal of the Gettysburg website and some of the off-site links, we noticed that the number of annual visitors to the park decreased significantly in the recent past. From 1980 to 2017, the park hosted over a million visitors per year. From 2018 to today, the park has seen roughly 750,000 visitors per year (NPS Stats, 2026). This drop seems particularly unusual given the relatively lower number of visitors persisted through the pandemic, despite the park being an open area for socializing and gathering. To increase the number of visitor, purely through changes to the website and not an advertising campaign, we recommend making the park-specific rules under the Superintendent’s Compendium easier to read, enticing people with how popular the park can be by highlighting park statistics, and by making accessibility information easier to find so that potential visitors with disabilities are not discouraged from making the trip.

Part 4: Conclusion

The Gettysburg National Military Park website has already shown great potential. NPS is communicating an enormous amount of information on one website. The website still has a few areas that lack well organized information. The accessibility and safety policies were not well laid out. The park-specific rules are dense and unwieldy and park statistics are not available on the park’s website. We are proposing greater consistency between the two Visitors Center websites, a more modern format for the Superintendent’s Compendium, and additional transparency by including some basic visitor statistics on the park’s website. We believe these three relatively small changes will increase the usability of the Gettysburg website and, in turn, increase the number of visitors the park receives each year.
